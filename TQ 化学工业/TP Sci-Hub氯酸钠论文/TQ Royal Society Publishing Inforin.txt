    On heating,sodium chlorate monocrystals undergo a limited degree ofthermal decomposition in the solid state (0.1-1 mass loss)between450 K and the melting point,ca.535 K.The degree to which this occursdepends on the perfection of the sample,the more perfect crystals sufferinglower mass loss.Examination of thermally treated pristine surfaces witha microscope reveals distributions of decomposition sites characteristic ofthe reaction initiating where emergent dislocations intersect the crystalsurface.There is little correlation,however,between dislocation etch-pitsand decomposition centres.
    X-ray topographic studies of changes in bulk substructure duringthermal treatment confirm that 'grown in'dislocations play little or nopart in the nucleation of the decomposition process in the bulk.There issome evidence,however,that they may contribute to the surface reaction.
    In the bulk,the potential nuclei are strained regions in the lattice.Fromthese,and possibly as a consequence of decomposition,dislocation loopsare punched out into the surrounding lattice along (100>and (110>directions.Similar defects are emitted from localized regions at thesurface and it is possible that these give rise to the surface decompositionpatterns.Impurity doped crystals show considerably enhanced decom-position

    3.RESULTS
    3.1.Preliminary thermal examination
    Recrystallization of sodium chlorate from aqueous solution yields a cubic form(I)of space group P213 with four NaClOs per unit cell (Aravindakshan 1959;
    Ramachandran Chandrasekaran 1957).Studies of the phase behaviour of thismaterial made in conjunction with crystallographic measurements (Meyer 1972;
    Meyer Gosperin 1973;Meyer Rimsky 1979)have indicated that the solidretains this structure on heating to the melting point (535 K).In contrast,recrystallization from the melt at various cooling rates was reported to yield amore complicated phase behaviour.On slow cooling,the melt crystallized atca.535K to a monoclinic form (III)related structurally to I by a (110>sliptranslation.This transformation,at 503 K,proceeded so readily that no latticedisruption occurred and the crystal maintained its singularity.Supercooling of themelt to less than 528 K yielded a second form (II-uncharacterized),which againtransformed to phase I at 503 K.Additionally,rapid supercooling of the melt toless than 503 K resulted in the direct formation of phase I.Since the possibleoccurrence of a series of phase transformations in the solid state could influenceour experiments,we attempted to confirm these observations by differentialscanning calorimetry.

    3.2.Surface examinations
    Previous attempts to correlate bulk solid-state thermal decomposition withdislocations have relied on surface etching studies to reveal the sites of emergentdislocations.The resulting etch-pits and their densities have been correlatedsubsequently with decomposition sites or with rates of decomposition (Thomas
    Renshaw 1967,1969;Herley et al.1970,197I).
    Etching of sodium chlorate (001 surfaces with aqueous ethanol at roomtemperature gave etch patterns as shown in figure 1a(plate 1).These are charac-teristic of a random distribution of growth dislocations(large pits)and mechanicallyinduced dislocations(small pits)emergent at the surface.The geometry of the pits(centre point bottoms)corresponds to a dislocation line normal to the surface,i.e.(001〉line direction.
    Since sodium chlorate does not cleave,the only tests that could be applied toconfirm the association of these etch pits with dislocations were that continuedetching yielded no significant change in the numbers or the distribution of pits on aparticular surface and that high stress deformation by indentation yieldedcharacteristic (100>and more occasionally (110>alignments (figure 16,c).Thesewere always few in number but,as we have noted previously (Hooper et al.1982),sodium chlorate shows a dominantly brittle behaviour at temperatures below
    420-470K.
    The combination of dislocation line direction ((100>)and slip trace alignmentsdefines (100)and (110)as the most likely slip planes and indicates the possibleslip systems {100010>and {110(110>edge or (100>and (110>screw.This isconsistent with the results of previous and current topographic studies (Hooperet al.1982).For energetic reasons,dislocations with b (100>should dominate.

    4.DISCUSSION
    Our preliminary examination showed that crystals of sodium chlorate undergoa limited two-stage decomposition process:the first stage occurring at ca.420 Kand the last at ca.520 K,coupled with a noticeable deterioration of the crystalsurface.The degree to which these changes occurred was associable with the degreeof perfection of the crystal.The initial topographic study confirmed that there areparallel changes in substructure and that these are more significant in the moredefective and impure crystals.We note that we do not observe parallel changes onheating crystals of materials that do not undergo decomposition.It is of interestto characterize the nature of the developing defect structure.
