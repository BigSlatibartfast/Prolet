    I.Introduction
    "At any time and without warning,a submarine may have to remain submerged for several days on accountof the presence of the enemy,or rough weather,or serious accident to the machinery.Fortunately suchoccurrences are rare;but every commanding officer must be prepared to meet such an emergency that willafford his men the greatest possible chance of survival."
    hat quotation is taken from a review of submarine air purification technology published by the U.S.Bureau of
    Medicine and Surgery in 1919.At that time,the U.S.Navy had just begun to experiment with possible airpurification devices and supplies of oxygen that might permit submarines to remain submerged longer than theuntreated closed atmosphere would allow.Submariners were exposed to elevated levels of carbon dioxide andreduced levels of oxygen that would be considered completely unacceptable by current standards.It was a differentworld,but humans are still humans,and the requirements for safe and effective functioning in a self-containedenvironment are really unchanged.The maximum submergence time for submarines as published in that work wasapproximately 48 hours.
    In early submarines,the preferred supply of oxygen was 1800 psig compressed air bled into the boat asneeded..2 In order to maintain rough pressure control,while compressed air was bled in,"vitiated"air (air withreduced oxygen and elevated carbon dioxide)from the opposite end of the boat was compressed into waiting emptyvessels'.The periodic use of pressurized air to control oxygen caused swings in ambient pressure that wasuncomfortable for the crew.The need for added air or oxygen was occasionally "measured"by the physiologicalimpact on the crew rather than a reliable instrument.'The design of the submarine oxygen supply was limited toapproximately 25 day submerged operation.2 It was not until 1958 that U.S.submarines were able to carry out divesbeyond that period and necessitated new sources of oxygen.As early as 1919,liquid oxygen was a commerciallyavailable product and its use on submarines was contemplated.
    As submarines were required to accomplish longer and longer submerged periods of operation,the need tomanage normal and emergency life support supplies became a priority.Sodium chlorate is a very efficient means ofstoring oxygen with a mass of oxygen per unit of volume greater than compressed oxygen (unless the pressure isabove roughly 4,000 psig).The volume efficiency of candles is almost equal to that of liquid oxygen without thedangers and equipment issues of cryogenic storage.3.4 These devices are known by several names:Oxygen Candles,
    Chlorate Candles,SCOG (either Solid Chemical Oxygen Generator or Self-Contained Oxygen Generator)andSFOG (pronounced "S-FOG,Solid Fuel Oxygen Generator).
    The use of chlorates and perchlorates as sources of oxygen dates from at least 1930 when German firms offeredemergency oxygen supplies for use in mines.5.6 During World War II,the Japanese introduced a chemical oxygengenerator for aircraft pilot use.Both of those devices had serious shortcomings because they generated oxygencontaminated with excessive amounts of chlorine and carbon monoxide.3.6 The early Japanese candles actually usedcarbon as the sacrificial energy source leading to much higher amounts of carbon monoxide and dioxide beingproduced.By 1945,sodium chlorate oxygen candles had been improved and had been tested aboard the USS
    Sailfish.3 The candles were developed in part by the Naval Research Laboratory (NRL),5 and the Oldbury Electro-
    Chemical Corporation.5.6 Candles were considered a supplementary oxygen supply to the high-pressure oxygen

    II.Chemicals and Chemistry of Solid Oxygen Sources
    The most common commercial and military chemical oxygen generating candles are composed primarily ofsodium chlorate,NaCIO3.This material melts at approximately 248C(478F)and decomposes at 478C (892F).
    Other chemicals that may be used alone or combined are sodium perchlorate (NaClO4),potassium chlorate (KCIO3),potassium perchlorate (KC1O4),lithium chlorate (LiClO3),and lithium perchlorate (LiClO4).Table 1 lists the mostcommon materials that have been or could be used as a source of chemically generated oxygen along with theirmelting and decomposition temperatures.The release of oxygen from either chlorates or perchlorates requiresraising the material to substantial temperatures.As seen in Table 2,the decomposition reactions are mildlyexothermic,but an additional energy source is generally required to form a sustained reaction.Increasing thereaction temperature increases the rate of oxygen production.
    III.Impurities in Oxygen Product
    The presence of some carbon in the iron (or steel fibers)generates both carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide.
    The use of reduced iron powder as opposed to steel wool lowers the amount of both carbon gases.Early U.S.NAVYfurnaces were equipped with Hopcalite filters to convert the more hazardous carbon monoxide to carbon dioxide.6
    The current British SCOGs have an internal Hopcalite (Moleculite)filter1.Hopcalite acts as a catalyst for thecarbon monoxide conversion and is not consumed in use.

    IV.Manufacturing Processes
    The candle manufacturing process involved thoroughly mixing the dry ingredients,moistening with 1.5%wt to5%wt3 water and pressing with a high pressure ram.The higher the ram pressure the higher the final candle density.NRL performed tests from 5,000 to 24,000 psi.From 5,000 to 12,000 psig the density of the candle rose from 2.1 to
    2.27 g/ml (an 8%increase).Increasing to 24,000 psig yielded a density of 2.4 g/ml (a further 6%increase).Thedensity of crystalline sodium chlorate is approximately 2.7 g/ml.
    In order to remove the water,the final candles had to be thoroughly heated,without raising the exteriortemperature too high.The original manufacturing process dried the candles at 100C.5 Poor heat transfer within thecandle led to long drying times of up to eight days.Drying at 235C caused roughly 25%of the barium peroxide to

    V.Ignition of Candles
    The ignition of the candles is aided by a high concentration of iron in an igniter zone.The proportion of reducediron in that zone may be 20%wt to 30%wt or higher.Some candles also incorporate an elevated iron "booster"section across the entire top of the candle surrounding the igniter zone.This booster section serves to evenly ignitethe cross section of the candle.Ignition is accomplished by a high energy source.Sufficient energy is required toraise the temperature of a local area to the decomposition point.Several methods have typically been used:
    A.Red phosphorous nail
    Ignition by a red phosphorus tipped nail was employed as early as 1950.The U.S.NAVY igniter "match"is asix inch long,nine gauge foundry nail.The head of such a nail is approximately 1/2 inch in diameter.The nail headis coated with red phosphorous by painting or dipping the head into phosphorus dissolved in various mixtures ofglue and acetone.The phosphorous igniter was found to be reliable at room temperature.For aircraft devices,thephosphorous proved to be inconsistent at very low temperatures(-50C).3
    B.Cartridge,Standard 0.410 Bore
    The British SCOG utilizes a bullet-less 0.410 bore cartridge and striker.8 The burning debris from the cartridgeimpacts the ignition end of the candle,or "brick,"and begins the burn.
    C.Thermite
    National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA)has a flight qualified chemical oxygen generatorsimilar to the SCOG,designated the SFOG.Because of the NOx emissions associated with the cartridge igniter.NASA developed an igniter in the same cartridge utilizing a mixture of aluminum and magnesium powders and ironoxide.The cartridge primer,ignited by a physical strike,sets the exothermic reaction off.This type of ignitergenerated much lower amounts of NOx.

    B.NFPA 495,"Explosive Materials Code,"27 specifically deals with manufacturers of explosive materials andtherefore does not apply directly to manufacturers of oxygen generators.However,a number of the requirementsand suggestions are valuable for consideration
    C.NFPA 1124,"Code for Manufacture,Transport,and Storage of Fireworks and Pyrotechnic Articles,"28 andNFPA 1125,"Code for Manufacture of Model Rocket and High Power Rocket Motors,"29 provide usefulinformation although oxygen candles are not considered pyrotechnics nor rocket motors.The similarity lies in thefact that oxygen candles,pyrotechnics and solid rocket motors contain both fuel and oxidizer mixtures.

    XI.Conclusions
    A.The U.S.NAVY Oxygen Candle Furnace is constructed of materials that are compatible with its intendedexposure to high concentrations of oxygen at elevated temperatures.The required conditions are eased by the lowpressures expected during operation.
    B.The current U.S.NAVY Oxygen Candle Furnace will safely contain fires and pressures resulting from anycredible level of accidental oil contamination of the candles.The crew is instructed to visually inspect all candlesprior to loading them into the furnace.Since hydrocarbon contamination is the only known scenario that can triggera candle fire,the U.S.Oxygen Candle Furnace should be considered safe as currently used by the U.S.submarineforce.The basket,furnace,and lid were not damaged in these tests.Should a similar shipboard event occur,the crewcould continue to burn candles in the same furnace.The residue from the contaminated candles may be difficult toremove from the furnace.Sailors are instructed to attempt to chip or pry the clinker material.If chipping provesdifficult,they are instructed to use water to loosen or dissolve the hard material.
    Contamination of the candle by wicking hydrocarbon up through the bottom has been shown to slow downconsiderably after approximately 80 ml had been absorbed through the entire bottom surface.Oil wicking upthrough a pin-hole in the candle can would be a slow process and may be limited in the maximum amount of oil thatcan be absorbed.
    The limit of 91 ml of oil contaminant is most likely a conservative limit.Some additional contamination shouldbe able to be handled without damaging the furnace or its component parts.(The large volumes of contaminantsused in the 1985 NRL tests,which destroyed the furnace baskets,are not considered a credible accidentalcontamination scenario.)The function of the relief valve did not come into play with these tests since the relief
    13

    pressure was not reached.Past tests by NRL showed that the flow from the relief valve is sufficient to preventpressure increases that could eject the furnace lid.The precise limit of contaminant that can be handled withoutdamaging the furnace or basket is not known.
    C.The current U.S.sodium chlorate candles will operate effectively and safely at pressures up to 5 atm.Theamount of oxygen generated will be unchanged from operation at ambient pressure.The candles will burn slightlyfaster at elevated pressures,and the clinker may appear different to the operator.The clinker should be able to beremoved from the furnace basket as easily as those burned at ambient pressure.The candle furnace will not bedamaged by operation at elevated pressure.
